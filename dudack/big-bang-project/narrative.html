Part III

The Narrative

The whole story from start to finish, written like an actual story instead of a list of facts. Click the icons to see what happens. Seven chapters.

Chapter 1
Before Everything

There was no space, no time, no matter. Not even darkness, because darkness needs space to exist in. This isn't poetic language, it's just physics. Then 13.8 billion years ago something we still can't fully explain happened. Space came into existence. With it came an incomprehensible amount of energy packed into a region smaller than a proton. That was the beginning of everything. Including time.

Chapter 2
The First Second

In the first tiny fraction of a second the universe expanded violently, faster than light, in a burst called inflation. It went from subatomic to grapefruit-sized almost instantly. The temperature was beyond anything we have real words for. A billion billion billion degrees. And all that energy started doing something: it started turning into matter.

Thanks to E = mc², energy and mass are interchangeable. So the universe kept popping out particles, always in pairs, one matter and one antimatter, which would annihilate each other right back into energy. Over and over. But something was slightly off. For every billion antimatter particles there was one extra matter particle. When everything settled, that tiny surplus was all that remained. That's us. That's everything.

Chapter 3
Three Minutes That Wrote the Recipe

By the first second quarks had cooled enough to stick together into protons and neutrons. Then for about three minutes the universe was the perfect temperature for nuclear fusion. Protons and neutrons started fusing into helium nuclei. Then it cooled past the fusion point and that was it, process over. Ratio locked in: roughly 75% hydrogen, 25% helium.

That sounds like a minor chemistry detail but it's actually a big deal. The Big Bang model calculated that exact ratio from nuclear physics before anyone could measure it. When astronomers finally did measure the oldest gas in the universe they got the same number. That kind of prediction followed by confirmation is about as good as science gets.

Chapter 4
The Moment Light Was Set Free

For 380,000 years the universe was a fog. Photons existed but kept bouncing off free electrons and couldn't travel in a straight line. The whole thing was completely opaque.

Then the temperature dropped below 3,000 Kelvin. Electrons slowed down enough to bond with nuclei and form neutral atoms. The fog cleared, almost instantly. Light flooded out in every direction for the first time ever. We can still detect that light today. It's been traveling for 13.4 billion years and expansion has cooled it to 2.725 Kelvin. We call it the Cosmic Microwave Background and it fills the entire sky. It's the oldest thing humans have ever observed.

Chapter 5
Lights On

After the CMB was released the universe went dark again. No stars, no light, just massive clouds of hydrogen and helium drifting in silence. This is called the Cosmic Dark Ages and it lasted a couple hundred million years. Then gravity started doing what it always does. Gas clouds collapsed, pressure built, temperatures climbed. The first stars switched on.

These early Population III stars were nothing like our Sun. Hundreds of times more massive, much brighter, and they burned through their fuel in just a few million years. When they died they exploded and made something crucial: the first heavy elements. Carbon, oxygen, iron. They scattered those into space and that material became the building blocks for everything that came after.

Chapter 6
Galaxies, Stars, and Eventually Us

Over the next several billion years gravity organized everything into galaxies. Stars formed, lived, exploded, seeded the next generation. About 9.2 billion years after the Big Bang (4.6 billion years ago), a cloud of gas and old stellar debris collapsed to form the Sun. The leftover material clumped into planets. Earth formed.

Here's the part worth sitting with. The calcium in your bones, the iron in your blood, the oxygen you're breathing right now. All of it was made inside stars that exploded before the Sun existed. That's not a metaphor. That's just chemistry.

Chapter 7
Right Now

The universe is 13.8 billion years old and still expanding. Not just expanding, accelerating. Something called dark energy is pushing galaxies apart faster and faster and we don't really know what it is. Eventually, in the very far future, galaxies will be so distant that light from them won't reach us anymore. The universe will look empty even though it isn't.

Three totally seperate lines of evidence point to the same conclusion. Redshift shows the universe is expanding. The CMB shows it was once incredibly hot. The H/He ratio matches a prediction made from nuclear physics before anyone could check it. Three different methods, same answer every time. That's not coincidence.
